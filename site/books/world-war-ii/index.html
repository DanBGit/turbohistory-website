If you want one book that covers the whole war, read Antony Beevor's The Second World War. If you want the war as people lived it, read Max Hastings' Inferno. Every book listed here is real, in print, and genuinely worth the time. Reading times are honest estimates based on page count at roughly 250 words per minute.

The picks, in detail

1. The Second World War

Antony Beevor, 2012 · ~880 pages · About 25 hours · The single best one-volume history of the whole war

Beevor writes narrative history better than almost anyone alive. This is the book to read if you want one authoritative account that covers every theatre without turning into a textbook. It is long, but it never feels like homework.

2. Inferno: The World at War, 1939-1945

Max Hastings, 2011 · ~750 pages · About 21 hours · The war as ordinary people actually experienced it

Hastings keeps cutting from generals to civilians and back. If Beevor tells you what happened, Hastings tells you what it felt like, and he is unsentimental about which side did what.

3. The Rise and Fall of the Third Reich

William L. Shirer, 1960 · ~1,250 pages · About 35 hours · Understanding how Germany got there in the first place

Shirer was an American journalist in Berlin through the 1930s, so he watched a lot of this happen. Some of the scholarship has dated, but nothing else has that eyewitness authority.

4. Stalingrad

Antony Beevor, 1998 · ~500 pages · About 14 hours · The single battle that broke the German army

The book that made Beevor's name. If you only read about one campaign on the Eastern Front, where the war was really decided, read this one.

5. With the Old Breed

E. B. Sledge, 1981 · ~350 pages · About 9 hours · The Pacific war, from inside it

Sledge was a US Marine at Peleliu and Okinawa and wrote it down plainly afterwards. It is the least glamorous war book you will ever read, which is exactly why it is the truest.

6. Hiroshima

John Hersey, 1946 · ~150 pages · About 3 hours · The shortest book here that will stay with you longest

Hersey followed six survivors through the morning of 6 August 1945 and simply reported what happened to them. Published a year after the event. Still the standard by which this kind of writing is judged.

7. The Diary of a Young Girl

Anne Frank, 1947 · ~300 pages · About 7 hours · The human cost, in one voice

You probably met this at school and may have forgotten it is genuinely extraordinary writing by a genuinely extraordinary teenager. Read as an adult, it lands completely differently.

8. The Storm of War

Andrew Roberts, 2009 · ~700 pages · About 20 hours · Why Germany lost, argued rather than just narrated

Roberts has a thesis: Nazi ideology repeatedly overruled military sense, and that is what cost Hitler the war. Read it after Beevor, when you want an argument rather than an account.

Questions people actually ask

What is the single best book about World War 2?

For one book covering the entire war, Antony Beevor's The Second World War (2012). It is comprehensive, well sourced and genuinely readable. If 880 pages is not realistic right now, start with something shorter and come back to it.

Where should I start if I know nothing about WW2?

Do not start with a 900 page doorstop, because most people do not finish them. Start with a short overview to get the shape of the war, then pick the theatre or the person that grabbed you and go deep. Our own hour-long overview was built for exactly this, and the books above are where to go next.

What is the best short book about World War 2?

John Hersey's Hiroshima is about 150 pages and unforgettable, though it covers one event rather than the war. For a short overview of the whole conflict you are looking at either a Very Short Introduction or our own Turbo History, both of which take roughly an hour.

Should I read fiction or non-fiction about WW2?

Non-fiction first, if you want to actually understand it. The war does not need dramatising and the real accounts are stranger than the invented ones. Read the history, then the novels will land far harder.

Which WW2 book is best for the Eastern Front?

Antony Beevor's Stalingrad. The Eastern Front is where the majority of German casualties happened and where the war was effectively decided, and most Western readers underestimate it badly.
