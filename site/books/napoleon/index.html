The Best Napoleon Books: Where To Actually Start

If you want one full biography, read Andrew Roberts's Napoleon: A Life, which is the most readable of the big modern books and the most admiring. If you want the same life told by someone far less impressed, read Adam Zamoyski's Napoleon: The Man Behind the Myth. If you want the wars rather than the man, David Chandler's The Campaigns of Napoleon is still the standard reference sixty years on. If you want it short, David Bell's Napoleon: A Concise Biography does the whole thing in about 139 pages. And if you have under an hour rather than a fortnight, ours covers the whole life in one sitting.

Every book listed here is real, in print or easily found in a library, and worth the time. Reading times are honest estimates from page count at roughly 250 words per minute, so treat them as rough. One warning specific to Napoleon: almost nothing written about him is neutral. Roberts is broadly an admirer and Zamoyski is broadly not, and both of them are working from good evidence. Read one of each if you can, and be careful with anything drawn heavily from the memoirs Napoleon dictated on Saint Helena, because those were written to build a legend and they succeeded.

The picks, in detail

1. Napoleon: A Life

Andrew Roberts, 2014 · ~926 pages · About 26 hours · The best readable one-volume life. Built on the full edition of Napoleon's 33,000 surviving letters

Roberts visited most of the battlefields and worked through the letters as they were published for the first time, and the result moves faster than a book this size has any right to. It is very good on Napoleon as an administrator rather than only a general. It is also openly sympathetic, it plays down the human cost of the wars, and the UK edition is titled Napoleon the Great, which tells you the argument before you open it.

2. Napoleon: The Man Behind the Myth

Adam Zamoyski, 2018 · ~752 pages · About 21 hours · The corrective to Roberts. A deflating account by a historian who thinks the legend has eaten the evidence

Zamoyski went back to European archives rather than the well-worn French ones and comes out with a man who was often insecure, improvising and lucky. It is the best single book for stripping off the myth-making, including Napoleon's own. It is unsparing to the point of being cold, so if you want to understand why anyone followed him it is not the place to start.

3. Napoleon ours

Turbo History · ours · ~5,400 words · Under an hour · The whole life in one sitting. How a Corsican outsider took France, what he built, and what it cost

Every book on this list goes deeper than ours, and most of them are hard work. That is not a criticism, it is the entire point of them. Roberts gives you 926 pages built on 33,000 letters. Chandler's Campaigns of Napoleon runs to about 1,172 pages of battle analysis and maps. They are good books, and they want weeks of your life.

If you are studying the period, or writing about it, or sitting an exam on it, buy one of those and give it the time it deserves. We are not pretending to compete with them.

If you are not, ours might suit you better. It starts on Corsica, an island nobody in Paris much wanted, with a boy sent to school in France at nine who was mocked for his accent and lost his father young. Then the rise, the expansion, the years of domination and the fall, in four clear stages. Then the part most short accounts skip: what actually survived him. The Napoleonic Code, still under the law of France, Belgium, Luxembourg, Monaco, Louisiana and Quebec. The prefects, the Council of State, the Banque de France, the baccalaureate, the metric system. German and Italian nationalism, which he created by accident and did not want. And then the ledger that does not fit on a monument: slavery reinstated in the French Caribbean in 1802, rigged plebiscites, censored newspapers, women's rights cut back by his own code, museums full of looted art, and roughly six million dead. It ends where he did, on Saint Helena in 1821, with the systems still running.

That is what the whole series is for. Love history, but hate how it was taught at school? Same. School turned it into a memory test: names, dates, family trees, exam. The actual story never stood a chance. Turbo History is one figure or one event per book, told in under an hour. The rise, the fall, and why it still matters. Dates included, memorising optional. 4. Napoleon: The Path to Power, 1769-1799

Philip Dwyer, 2007 · ~651 pages · About 19 hours · The scholarly deep dive on the rise. Thirty years, examined properly, before he ever took the throne

Dwyer spends an entire volume on the years most biographies rush through, and he is excellent on how the young Bonaparte manufactured his own reputation through the press and through his bulletins. It is the first of two volumes, with Citizen Emperor covering 1799 to 1815, so committing to it is committing to well over a thousand pages. It is also a scholarly book and reads at that pace.

5. The Campaigns of Napoleon

David G. Chandler, 1966 · ~1,172 pages · About 33 hours · The military reference, not a biography. Every campaign, with maps, from Toulon to Waterloo

This has been the standard English-language work on Napoleonic warfare for sixty years and nothing has really replaced it. If you want to understand what Napoleon actually did differently on a battlefield, this is the book. It is a reference work rather than a read, some of the scholarship has dated, and it says almost nothing about politics, marriage or motive.

6. The Napoleonic Wars: A Global History

Alexander Mikaberidze, 2020 · ~936 pages · About 27 hours · The specialist angle worth the effort. The wars as a world conflict rather than a European one

Mikaberidze puts the fighting in the Americas, the Ottoman Empire, Iran, India and the oceans back into a story usually told as if it happened between Paris and Moscow, and it changes the scale of the thing entirely. It won the Society for Military History's book award and deserved it. It is not a biography of Napoleon and he disappears for long stretches, which is the point but can be disorienting.

7. Moscow 1812: Napoleon's Fatal March

Adam Zamoyski, 2004 · ~672 pages · About 19 hours · One campaign, told at full length. The invasion that destroyed the army and started the end

Zamoyski built this out of soldiers' diaries and letters from both sides, and it is the most vivid account of the Russian campaign in English. If you only read one book about a single Napoleonic campaign, read this one. It is genuinely harrowing in the retreat chapters, and it assumes you already know who everyone is, so it works better as a second or third book than a first.

8. Napoleon: A Concise Biography

David A. Bell, 2015 · ~139 pages · About 4 hours · The fastest serious introduction. A leading historian of the period doing the whole life in an afternoon

Bell is a specialist in revolutionary France and this is the rare short book that does not simplify into nonsense. He is especially clear on how much of Napoleon's career was made possible by the Revolution rather than by his own genius. It is short enough that battles get a sentence each, so you will finish it wanting more detail, which is arguably the job.

Questions people actually ask

What is the best book about Napoleon?

Andrew Roberts's Napoleon: A Life (2014) is the best single-volume biography for most readers. It is built on the complete edition of Napoleon's letters, it covers the administration as well as the battles, and it is genuinely readable at 926 pages. Be aware that Roberts admires his subject, so pair it with Adam Zamoyski's Napoleon: The Man Behind the Myth if you want the other side of the argument.

Where should I start with Napoleon?

If you have the time, start with Roberts. If you do not, start with David Bell's Napoleon: A Concise Biography, which is about 139 pages and written by a serious historian of revolutionary France. Bell is particularly good on how much the Revolution had already changed before Napoleon arrived, which is the context most popular accounts leave out.

What is the best book on Napoleon's battles?

David Chandler's The Campaigns of Napoleon, first published in 1966, is still the standard work. It runs to about 1,172 pages and covers every campaign with maps. It is a reference book rather than a narrative, so use it alongside a biography rather than instead of one. For a single campaign told as a story, Adam Zamoyski's account of the 1812 invasion of Russia is the better read.

Are there books critical of Napoleon?

Yes, and you should read at least one. Adam Zamoyski's Napoleon: The Man Behind the Myth is the most prominent recent example, and it argues that the legend was largely manufactured, partly by Napoleon himself on Saint Helena. Alexander Mikaberidze's The Napoleonic Wars: A Global History is not a hostile book but it makes the scale of the damage much harder to ignore by looking beyond Europe.

What is the best short book about Napoleon?

David Bell's Napoleon: A Concise Biography, at roughly 139 pages, is the best short book by a specialist. It gives you the shape of the career without pretending the battles can be covered properly in that space. Our own hour-long version is shorter again and focuses on what he built and what it cost rather than on campaign detail.
